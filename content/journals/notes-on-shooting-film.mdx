I switched to film photography three years ago, not for nostalgia or aesthetics — though both followed — but because I was taking too many photographs and paying attention to none of them.

Digital had given me infinite latitude. Unlimited frames, instant review, easy deletion. I was documenting everything and experiencing almost nothing. The camera had become a way to feel like I was *having* a moment without actually being present in it.

## 36 Exposures

A roll of 35mm gives you 36 frames. Some rolls give you 24. That constraint changes everything.

You don't spray and pray. You wait. You think about the light before you raise the camera. You compose in your head before you compose in the viewfinder. You develop an intuition for when something is worth pressing the shutter — not because you'll run out of frames exactly, but because the scarcity trains a discipline that stays with you.

It's similar to what I've found in research: when you have unlimited time and budget, you tend to over-collect and under-synthesize. Constraints force prioritization. Prioritization forces judgment. Judgment is the actual skill.

## The Latency as Feature

You don't see film photographs immediately. There's a gap between taking the picture and seeing it — days, sometimes weeks, depending on how busy you are and when you get to the lab.

That latency turns out to be valuable. By the time you see the images, you've lost the emotional attachment to the decisions you made. You see the photograph more clearly because you're not defending your choice to take it. The gap creates the critical distance that lets you actually evaluate what you made.

I've started building artificial versions of this into my design work. I'll finish a prototype and deliberately not look at it for 48 hours before reviewing it. It's not the same thing. But the principle holds.

## What Film Gave Back

I take fewer photographs now. I pay more attention to what I'm taking. I have a box of prints I actually look at.

More than the aesthetics or the process, that's what I was after: a way of engaging with the world that didn't dissolve the experience into documentation. 

Film photographs the world the way memory does — selectively, and with a grain that makes the image feel like it was made, not captured.
