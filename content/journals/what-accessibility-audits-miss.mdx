The most common question I get when I tell someone I'm doing an accessibility audit: "Can't you just run a scanner?"

The short answer is yes, and you absolutely should. Tools like axe-core, Lighthouse, and WAVE are fast, comprehensive within their scope, and catch real issues. They're table stakes.

The longer answer is that automated tools catch approximately 30% of actual accessibility failures. The rest require human judgment, assistive technology testing, and the kind of contextual knowledge about how people actually use these systems that no scanner can replicate.

## What Scanners Are Good At

Automated tools are excellent at catching structural issues:

- Missing alt text on images
- Form inputs without associated labels
- Insufficient color contrast ratios
- Missing landmark regions
- Pages without proper heading hierarchy
- Links without descriptive text

These are the issues that have clear, machine-checkable pass/fail criteria. They're also disproportionately the issues that developers are most likely to inadvertently introduce. Running a scanner in CI is genuinely useful.

## What Scanners Miss

**Dynamic content**: Scanners take a snapshot. They don't test what happens when a modal opens, a form error appears, or content loads asynchronously. In complex applications — and most applications are complex — dynamic behavior is where the hardest accessibility failures live.

**Focus management**: Where does keyboard focus go when a modal closes? When a dialog confirms an action? When an accordion expands? Scanners can't tell you whether focus is managed correctly because focus management is inherently about sequence and interaction, not static state.

**Cognitive load and comprehension**: Screen reader users hear page content linearized and read aloud. A page that looks clean visually can be deeply confusing aurally — out-of-order reading flow, ambiguous button labels that only make sense visually, tables that convey meaning through color rather than structure. None of this is machine-checkable.

**Task completion**: The ultimate accessibility question isn't "does this element have a label?" — it's "can this person actually do the thing they came to do?" That requires testing with real users who use assistive technology to accomplish real tasks. No scanner tells you whether a screen reader user can successfully complete a checkout flow.

## What Good Audits Actually Involve

When I run an accessibility audit, automated scanning is the first step, not the whole process:

1. **Automated scan** across all key pages and interaction states
2. **Expert heuristic review** against WCAG 2.1 AA criteria, focused on interaction patterns
3. **Keyboard-only navigation** through all user flows
4. **Screen reader testing** with at least two readers (I use NVDA and VoiceOver)
5. **Moderated sessions with AT users** for critical flows (booking, checkout, forms)

The combination catches issues that no single method reaches.

## The 30% Problem

The dangerous thing about automated-only audits is that they can create a false impression of compliance. You run the scanner, it returns zero violations, and someone in the organization concludes "we passed accessibility."

What you actually have is evidence that your page structure is technically valid. That's necessary but not sufficient. The experience of using that page with a screen reader, or a switch device, or under the cognitive load of a complex task — that's entirely untested.

The 30% you catch with automation is worth catching. But frame it correctly: it's a floor, not a ceiling.

---

*If you're building a roadmap for accessibility work: automated scanning is an excellent ongoing practice for catching regressions. Invest in moderated AT testing at least once per year, and especially before major redesigns.*
