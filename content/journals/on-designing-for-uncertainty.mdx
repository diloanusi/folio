There's a particular kind of project that starts with a brief and ends with a completely different question. You come in thinking you're solving a navigation problem. Six weeks of research later, you realize the navigation *is* the symptom — the underlying issue is that users don't trust the system enough to commit to a route.

This has happened to me enough times that I've started to treat the original brief as provisional. A hypothesis, not a contract.

## The Stable Problem Assumption

Most design processes — double diamond, design sprints, IDEO's human-centered framework — are built around the idea that you can define the problem clearly enough to solve it. Diverge, converge. Research, synthesize, ideate, prototype.

The implicit assumption is that the problem is stable long enough for you to address it.

But in practice, especially in complex systems (healthcare, transit, finance), the problem shifts as you learn more. The map changes because the territory was never fixed to begin with.

## Designing for the Drift

What I've found useful is treating early-stage research less like problem-scoping and more like *orientation*. You're not trying to nail the problem. You're trying to understand the landscape well enough to notice when you've moved.

Practically, this means:
- Keeping synthesis artifacts living and revisable, not final deliverables
- Checking your original research questions at the midpoint and asking whether they're still the right questions
- Explicitly noting when a finding changes your understanding of the problem, not just your understanding of the solution

It also means being comfortable saying, mid-project: *we've learned that we were solving the wrong thing.* That's not a failure. It's what good research looks like.

## The Brief as a Starting Position

The brief is where you begin, not where you're going. The clients who understand this tend to get the most from research. They're not threatened when the question changes — they're curious about what the new question reveals.

The clients who don't can make excellent work difficult. They've already decided what the answer is; they hired research to validate it. That's a different kind of project, and worth naming clearly at the start.

---

I've started adding a line to my project kickoffs: *"This brief is our current best guess at the problem. We'll treat it as a living document."* It's a small reframe, but it's changed how teams receive shifting findings more than any other single thing I've tried.
